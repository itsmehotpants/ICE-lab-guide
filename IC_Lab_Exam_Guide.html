EXP 01 Cut-Section Model of 4-Stroke Diesel Engine — Valve Timing Diagram ▼
AIM & APPARATUS

Aim: To draw the valve timing diagram for the given 4-stroke diesel engine.


Apparatus: Measuring tape, Scale, Thread, Feeler gauge

THEORY — The Golden Formula
Required Angle Calculation
Angle (°) = (Distance from Dead Centre × 360) / Circumference of Flywheel

where:
• Distance = linear distance on flywheel from TDC or BDC mark to the event position
• Circumference = measured using thread/measuring tape around flywheel
🧠 Easy Memory Trick

Think of the flywheel as a circle. You're converting a LENGTH on its rim into a DEGREE angle. It's just: "Fraction of circumference × 360°"

STEP-BY-STEP PROCEDURE (How to do it)
  1. 1

    Rotate the flywheel slowly and find TDC (Top Dead Centre) — the point where the piston is at its highest. Mark it on the flywheel with chalk/thread.

  2. 2

    Similarly find and mark BDC (Bottom Dead Centre) — piston at lowest point.

  3. 3

    Wrap a thread around the flywheel and measure the full circumference. (Your notebook value: 92 cm)

  4. 4

    Rotate the flywheel again and watch the inlet valve. The moment it begins to open — mark that position on the flywheel. This is IVO.

  5. 5

    Continue rotating until inlet valve fully closes — mark IVC.

  6. 6

    Repeat for exhaust valve — mark EVO (opens) and EVC (closes).

  7. 7

    Use thread to measure distance (cm) of each mark from its respective dead centre (TDC or BDC).

  8. 8

    Apply formula: Angle = (Distance × 360) / 92 for each event.

  9. 9

    Draw the valve timing diagram — a circle with TDC at top, BDC at bottom, angles marked for all 4 events.

Draw a circle. TDC at 12 o'clock, BDC at 6 o'clock. Mark angles clockwise. IVO is just after TDC, EVC is just after TDC (overlap region). IVC is just after BDC, EVO is just before BDC.

VIVA QUESTIONS & ANSWERS
    What is a valve timing diagram? ▼
    It is a circular diagram showing the opening and closing positions of inlet and exhaust valves in degrees of crank rotation, relative to TDC and BDC.
    What is TDC and BDC? ▼
    TDC (Top Dead Centre) is the position where the piston is at the highest point in the cylinder. BDC (Bottom Dead Centre) is where the piston is at the lowest point.
    Why does the inlet valve open before TDC? ▼
    To allow fresh charge to start filling the cylinder early, taking advantage of the inertia of the incoming air/fuel mixture. This improves volumetric efficiency.
    Why does the exhaust valve open before BDC? ▼
    To allow exhaust gases to start escaping before the piston reaches BDC, using the cylinder pressure to assist blow-down and ensure complete scavenging.
    What is valve overlap? ▼
    The period during which both inlet and exhaust valves are open simultaneously near TDC. It helps scavenge exhaust gases and charge the cylinder with fresh mixture.
    What is a feeler gauge used for? ▼
    A feeler gauge is used to measure the valve clearance (tappet clearance) — the gap between the rocker arm and valve stem. This ensures precise valve opening timing.
    Name the 4 strokes of a diesel engine. ▼
    1. Suction stroke (air enters), 2. Compression stroke (air compressed, fuel injected at end), 3. Power/Expansion stroke (combustion pushes piston down), 4. Exhaust stroke (burnt gases expelled).
EXP 02 4-Stroke Single Cylinder Diesel Engine — Test Rig with Electrical Dynamometer ▼
AIM & APPARATUS

Aim: To conduct a trial on 4-stroke diesel engine with electrical dynamometer and determine BP, BTE, BSFC, air-fuel ratio, and draw graphs.


Apparatus: 4-stroke diesel engine, electrical dynamometer, power supply, diesel, calorimeter, manometer (for air flow), burette (for fuel measurement)

KEY FORMULAS — Memorize These!
Brake Power (BP)
BP = (V × I × T) / 1000 kW    [Electrical Dyno]
OR   BP = 2πNT / 60000 kW    [Mechanical Dyno]
where V=Volts, I=Current(A), T=efficiency (or T=Torque in Nm, N=RPM)
Fuel Consumption (FC)
FC = (Volume of fuel × Specific gravity) / (Time in seconds × 1000)  kg/s
FC = (20 × 0.75) / (time × 1000)  kg/s  [for 20ml fuel, SG=0.75]
Brake Specific Fuel Consumption (BSFC)
BSFC = (FC × 3600) / BP    [kg/kWh]
Brake Thermal Efficiency (BTE)
BTE = BP / (FC × Cv) × 100    [%]
where Cv = Calorific Value of fuel = 44,000 kJ/kg for diesel
Mass of Air (Ma) — from manometer
Ma = Cd × A × √(2g × Ha × ρa/ρw)  kg/s
where Cd=0.62, A=area of orifice (d=14mm), Ha=manometer head, ρa=1.207 kg/m³
Air-Fuel Ratio = Ma / FC
STEP-BY-STEP PROCEDURE
  1. 1

    Check oil level in engine (use SAE-40 oil if low). Fill diesel in tank.

  2. 2

    Fill manometer with water to half height. Adjust water flow rate to engine and calorimeter.

  3. 3

    Fill burette with diesel by opening the valves (measure 20 ml fuel consumption).

  4. 4

    Start the engine, run at NO LOAD for 5 minutes until temperature stabilizes.

  5. 5

    Take first reading at no load: note V, I (=0), N (RPM), manometer H1 & H2, time for 20ml fuel, water flow rates.

  6. 6

    Apply LOAD using load bank (electrical load). Wait for temperature to stabilize.

  7. 7

    Note: Voltage (V), Current (I), RPM (N), manometer readings (H1, H2), time for 20ml fuel, temperatures T1–T5.

  8. 8

    Increase load in steps and repeat (typically 3–4 load readings).

  9. 9

    Record ALL readings in observation table.

  10. 10

    Calculate BP, FC, BSFC, BTE, Air-Fuel Ratio for each load.

  11. 11

    Draw graphs: BP vs Fuel Consumption (William's Line), BP vs BSFC (decreasing curve), BP vs BTE (increasing curve).

💡 Exam Tip — William's Line

William's Line is the graph of Fuel Consumption (y-axis) vs Brake Power (x-axis). It should be LINEAR. The x-intercept gives Friction Power (FP). This is a common viva question!

VIVA QUESTIONS & ANSWERS
    What is Brake Power? ▼
    Brake Power (BP) is the actual power available at the output shaft of the engine, measured using a dynamometer. It is less than indicated power due to friction losses. Formula: BP = 2πNT/60000 kW or BP = VIT/1000 kW.
    What is the difference between IP, BP, and FP? ▼
    IP (Indicated Power) = total power developed in cylinder. BP (Brake Power) = useful power at shaft. FP (Friction Power) = power lost to friction. Relation: IP = BP + FP. Mechanical Efficiency = BP/IP × 100.
    Why does BSFC decrease and then increase with load? ▼
    At low loads, the engine runs inefficiently (high BSFC). As load increases, efficiency improves (BSFC decreases). At very high loads, combustion becomes incomplete and mechanical losses increase, so BSFC rises again.
    What is BTE and its typical value for diesel engines? ▼
    Brake Thermal Efficiency (BTE) = BP/(FC × Cv) × 100. It represents how much of the fuel's heat energy is converted to useful work. Typical value for diesel engines: 25–40%. Your experiment gave ~27–37% at different loads.
    What is an electrical dynamometer? How does it work? ▼
    An electrical dynamometer absorbs engine power by generating electricity (acts as a generator). The load is applied by connecting electrical resistors. Power absorbed = V × I. It is easy to control and measure.
    What is specific gravity of diesel and its calorific value? ▼
    Specific gravity of diesel ≈ 0.75. Calorific value (Cv) ≈ 44,000 kJ/kg. These are standard values used in all fuel consumption and efficiency calculations.
    What is the purpose of the exhaust gas calorimeter? ▼
    The exhaust gas calorimeter measures the heat carried away by exhaust gases. It circulates cooling water and the temperature rise of water gives the heat rejected. Used in heat balance (energy audit) of the engine.
EXP 03 Multi-Cylinder Petrol Engine — Eddy Current Dynamometer (Morse Test) ▼
AIM

To calculate Indicated Power (IP), Friction Power (FP), and Mechanical Efficiency of a 4-stroke multi-cylinder petrol engine at full load and rated speed using Morse Test.

THEORY — The Morse Test Concept

The Morse Test is the only method to find IP of a multi-cylinder engine without using a pressure indicator. The idea is brilliantly simple:


→ Run engine at full load. Record BP (all cylinders firing).
→ Cut off one cylinder (short-circuit its spark plug).
→ Reduce dyno load to restore the SAME engine speed.
→ Record new BP. The DROP in BP = IP of that cylinder (because friction of that cylinder continues).

Morse Test Formulas
IP of cylinder n = BP(all firing) − BP(cylinder n cut off)

Total IP = IP₁ + IP₂ + IP₃ + IP₄

Mechanical Efficiency (η_mech) = BP / Total IP × 100%

Friction Power (FP) = Total IP − BP
🧠 Memory Trick

"Cut a cylinder, the BP drops. That drop IS the IP of that cylinder. Sum all drops = Total IP."

STEP-BY-STEP PROCEDURE
  1. 1

    Check fuel supply, lubrication oil, and cooling water availability before starting.

  2. 2

    Set dynamometer to zero load. Start engine, let it reach working temperature and steady state. Adjust to desired speed.

  3. 3

    Apply full load, note BP (all cylinders firing) = 5.75 kW

  4. 4

    Cut off cylinder 1 (short-circuit spark plug). Engine speed drops.

  5. 5

    REDUCE dynamometer load to restore original speed. Record new BP₁ = 4.64 kW

  6. 6

    Re-connect cylinder 1. Run all cylinders for steady state. Repeat for cylinder 2, 3, 4.

  7. 7

    Calculate IP for each cylinder, find total IP, then mechanical efficiency.

  8. 8

    Bring dyno load to zero, disengage and stop engine.

VIVA QUESTIONS & ANSWERS
    What is the principle of the Morse Test? ▼
    The Morse Test assumes friction power is independent of load and proportional to speed. When one cylinder is cut, the drop in BP (at constant speed) equals the IP of that cylinder, since its friction continues but its power contribution stops.
    Why is it important to maintain constant speed during Morse Test? ▼
    Because friction power is assumed constant only at constant speed. If speed changes, friction power changes, making the assumption invalid and the IP calculation inaccurate.
    Can Morse Test be applied to a single-cylinder engine? ▼
    No. Morse Test requires at least 2 cylinders. For a single cylinder engine, you cannot "cut" a cylinder — indicated power must be determined using a P-V diagram (indicator diagram).
    What is an eddy current dynamometer? ▼
    It uses electromagnetic induction. When the rotor (attached to engine shaft) rotates in a magnetic field, eddy currents are induced, creating a braking torque. The load is controlled by varying the magnetic field strength. Unlike electrical dynamometer, it dissipates energy as heat.
    What is mechanical efficiency and its typical value? ▼
    Mechanical efficiency = BP/IP × 100. It accounts for friction losses in piston rings, bearings, valve train etc. Typical values: 75–90% for modern engines. Your experiment: 72.78%.
EXP 04 Variable Compression Ratio (CRDI) Diesel Engine — Multiple Mode Tests ▼
AIM

To study the performance characteristics of a CRDI VCR diesel engine under: (1) Variable CR, (2) Constant speed, (3) Variable speed, (4) LPG/Dual fuel operation.

KEY CONCEPTS

CRDI (Common Rail Direct Injection): Fuel is supplied at HIGH pressure through a common rail to all injectors, ensuring better atomization, improved combustion, and reduced emissions.


VCR (Variable Compression Ratio): Allows changing CR while the engine is running by adjusting clearance volume. Change CR only at ZERO/low load.


Higher CR: Improves thermal efficiency, increases power, but may increase knocking.
Lower CR: Reduces knocking, suitable for high load operation.

Compression Ratio
CR = Total Cylinder Volume / Clearance Volume = (Vs + Vc) / Vc
where Vs = swept volume, Vc = clearance volume
STEP-BY-STEP PROCEDURE
  1. 1

    Check voltage (±12V), fuel level. Keep load at zero.

  2. 2

    Start water supply, set flow rates.

  3. 3

    Switch ON panel and battery charger.

  4. 4

    Start engine using ignition key.

  5. 5

    Open Neera and IC Engine software on computer.

  6. 6

    Set compression ratio using the CR adjustment mechanism.

  7. 7

    Apply load gradually and record: RPM, load, fuel consumption, cylinder pressure.

  8. 8

    Unload dynamometer completely then repeat for different CR or speed.

  9. 9

    For LPG test (Dual fuel mode): connect LPG, start diesel first, then switch to dual fuel mode.

RESULTS TREND (What to expect)
  • BP increases with load.
  • SFC decreases initially then increases at high load.
  • Thermal efficiency improves with higher CR.
  • Higher speed increases power output.
  • LPG operation reduces diesel consumption but requires careful control.
VIVA QUESTIONS & ANSWERS
    What is VCR engine and its advantage? ▼
    A VCR (Variable Compression Ratio) engine allows changing the compression ratio while running by altering the clearance volume. Advantages: optimizes efficiency at different loads, reduces emissions, allows dual-fuel operation, better performance across operating range.
    What is CRDI system? ▼
    Common Rail Direct Injection — fuel is stored at very high pressure (1000–2000 bar) in a common rail and injected directly into each cylinder. Advantages: precise fuel metering, better atomization, improved combustion efficiency, reduced NOx and particulate emissions.
    Why should CR never be changed at full load? ▼
    Changing CR at high load can cause mechanical damage to the engine. The connecting rod mechanism used to change clearance volume experiences excessive stress under high combustion pressures. Always change CR only at zero or low load.
    What are the benefits of dual fuel (LPG+Diesel) operation? ▼
    Reduced diesel consumption, lower particulate emissions, LPG burns cleaner than diesel, cost savings. Disadvantage: reduced power, risk of backfire, requires careful air-fuel ratio control.
EXP 05 Variable Compression Ratio Diesel Engine — Different CR & Loads ▼
AIM & OBJECTIVE

To study performance characteristics of a VCR diesel engine at different compression ratios and loads. Determine BP, SFC, thermal efficiency, and study the effect of CR on engine performance.

APPARATUS

Single cylinder VCR CRDI diesel engine, Eddy current dynamometer, fuel measuring burette, Air orifice unit, Calorimeter with water flow rotameter, Temp & pressure sensors, IC Engine & Neera software.

KEY FORMULAS (Air Compressor-Style Calculations)
From your notebook — Sample Calculations
h = (H1 − H2)/100 = 3.6×10⁻² m (manometer head)

ΔH = (ρw/ρa − 1) × h = (1000/1.21 − 1) × 3.6×10⁻² = 29.72 m

Qa = Cd × Ao × √(2g × ΔH)  [m³/s]

Qt = πd²LN / (60×4)  [theoretical air flow]

Volumetric Efficiency η_vol = Qa/Qt × 100%

Torque T = W × g × R (dyno arm radius = 0.18m)

Shaft Power Fs = 2πNT / (60×1000) kW
VIVA QUESTIONS
    How does compression ratio affect thermal efficiency? ▼
    Higher CR compresses air more, raising its temperature before fuel injection. This leads to better combustion, higher peak pressure, and more work output per cycle — thus higher thermal efficiency. For diesel: η_th = 1 − 1/CR^(γ−1) approximately.
    What is volumetric efficiency? ▼
    Volumetric efficiency = (Actual volume of air drawn in) / (Theoretical swept volume) × 100. It measures how effectively the engine "breathes". Affected by valve timing, intake temperature, engine speed, and cylinder geometry.
    What is isothermal efficiency of a compressor? ▼
    Isothermal efficiency = Isothermal power / Actual shaft power × 100. Isothermal compression is the ideal (most efficient) compression where temperature remains constant. Higher isothermal efficiency means the compressor approaches ideal behavior.
EXP 06 Double Stage Air Compressor Test Rig ▼
AIM & APPARATUS

Aim: To find volumetric efficiency, isothermal efficiency, and compression ratio of a double-stage air compressor.


Apparatus: Electric supply, water supply (2 LPM at 1 bar), double-stage air compressor with intercooler, pressure gauges, orifice meter for air measurement, rotameter.

THEORY — Two-Stage Compression

In a two-stage compressor, air is first compressed in a low-pressure (LP) cylinder, passed through an intercooler (where it is cooled back to near ambient temperature), then compressed again in a high-pressure (HP) cylinder.


The intercooling reduces the work required for second-stage compression, improving efficiency compared to single-stage compression to the same final pressure.

Key Formulas
Compression Ratio r = P_delivery / P_atmospheric

Isothermal Power (F_iso) = Pa × Qa × ln(r) / 1000  kW

Shaft Power (Fs) = 2πNT / 60000 kW (from motor input or torque)

Volumetric Efficiency η_vol = Qa / Qt × 100%

Isothermal Efficiency η_iso = F_iso / Fs × 100%
STEP-BY-STEP PROCEDURE
  1. 1

    Close delivery valve V1 of tank. Start the compressor.

  2. 2

    Connect cooling water supply to condenser outlet; open condenser cooling water to drain.

  3. 3

    Open valve V2 and start cooling water supply.

  4. 4

    Let receiver pressure rise to ~1 kg/cm². Now partially open V1 to maintain constant delivery pressure.

  5. 5

    Apply load on rod to maintain horizontal position during operation.

  6. 6

    Record: delivery pressure, orifice manometer readings (H1, H2), temperatures (T1–T4), load on balance, RPM.

  7. 7

    Repeat for different delivery pressures by adjusting V1.

SAMPLE RESULTS (From Notebook)
η_vol = 0.34% → (actually 34% — the decimal notation used in notebook)
r (compression ratio) = 303
η_iso = 62.81%
VIVA QUESTIONS & ANSWERS
    What is the purpose of the intercooler? ▼
    The intercooler cools the compressed air between stages, reducing its temperature back toward ambient. This reduces the volume and temperature of air entering the 2nd stage, reducing the work required for compression and improving overall efficiency.
    Why is isothermal compression the ideal case? ▼
    Isothermal compression (constant temperature) requires the least work of all compression processes. In practice, compression is polytropic (between isothermal and adiabatic). The isothermal efficiency measures how close the actual process is to the ideal isothermal process.
    What is clearance volume and how does it affect volumetric efficiency? ▼
    Clearance volume is the volume remaining in the cylinder when the piston is at TDC (dead end). At end of delivery, compressed gas remains in clearance volume and re-expands on suction stroke, reducing the fresh air intake — thus reducing volumetric efficiency. Higher clearance = lower η_vol.
    What is the advantage of 2-stage over single-stage compression? ▼
    Two-stage with intercooling: (1) Less total work input, (2) Lower delivery temperature, (3) Better volumetric efficiency, (4) Less thermal stress on components, (5) Can achieve higher pressure ratios safely.
EXP 07 Redwood Viscometer — Effect of Temperature on Viscosity ▼
AIM & APPARATUS

Aim: To analyse the effect of temperature on the viscosity of a fluid (oil) using Redwood Viscometer.


Apparatus: Redwood Viscometer No.1, oil sample, measuring flask (50 ml), stopwatch, thermometer, water bath.

THEORY

Viscosity = the property of a fluid that determines its resistance to flow. Lower the viscosity → greater the flow ability.


Redwood viscometer is used for low-viscous oils (flow time 30s–2000s) like kerosene oil, mustard oil. It determines viscosity at 80°C and below.


Key relationship: Viscosity is INVERSELY proportional to temperature — as temperature increases, viscosity decreases (oil thins).

  3. 3

  4. 4

    Place measuring flask (50 ml) below the orifice.

  5. 5

    Open the ball valve and start stopwatch simultaneously.

  6. 6

    Stop the watch when 50 ml of oil has collected in the flask.

  7. 7

    Repeat the reading and take average time (Redwood seconds).

  8. 8

    Convert time to kinematic viscosity using the formula.

  9. 9

    Repeat for different temperatures.

VIVA QUESTIONS & ANSWERS
    Define viscosity. What are its units? ▼
    Viscosity is the property of a fluid that resists relative motion between its layers (internal friction). Dynamic viscosity unit: Pa·s or poise (P). Kinematic viscosity = dynamic/density, unit: m²/s or centistokes (cSt = mm²/s).
    What is the effect of temperature on viscosity of liquids vs gases? ▼
    For LIQUIDS: viscosity DECREASES with temperature (oil thins when heated — intermolecular forces weaken). For GASES: viscosity INCREASES with temperature (molecular activity increases). This is the opposite behavior!
    What is Redwood No.1 vs Redwood No.2 viscometer? ▼
    Redwood No.1 is used for low-viscosity oils (flow time 30–2000s). Redwood No.2 has a larger orifice, used for heavy/viscous oils (flow time > 2000s). The lab uses Redwood No.1 for lubricating oils.
    Why is viscosity important for lubricating oils? ▼
    Viscosity determines the oil film thickness between moving parts. Too high: excessive friction and energy loss. Too low: oil film breaks, causing metal-to-metal contact and wear. Correct viscosity (SAE grade) must match the operating temperature range.
    What is the effect of pressure on viscosity? ▼
    Viscosity of liquids INCREASES with pressure (molecules are forced closer together, increasing resistance to flow). This is especially important in high-pressure hydraulic systems and engine lubrication.
EXP 08 Density Determination by Pycnometer ▼
AIM & APPARATUS

Aim: To determine the density of a given liquid using Pycnometer.


Apparatus: Pycnometer (specific gravity bottle), weighing machine, distilled water, test liquid.

THEORY

A Pycnometer (specific gravity bottle) is a glass flask with a close-fitting ground glass stopper with a fine capillary hole. It gives a precise, repeatable volume of liquid.


The density of liquid is found by comparing its mass to the mass of the same volume of water (known density).

Density Calculation
Let: m₀ = mass of empty dry pycnometer
     m₁ = mass of pycnometer + water (m₁ − m₀ = mass of water)
     m₂ = mass of pycnometer + test liquid (m₂ − m₀ = mass of liquid)

Volume of water = (m₁ − m₀) / ρ_water   [same volume for liquid]

Density of liquid ρ = (m₂ − m₀) / (m₁ − m₀) × ρ_water
STEP-BY-STEP PROCEDURE
  1. 1

    Weigh the empty, dry, clean pycnometer → m₀

  2. 2

    Fill with distilled water (up to brim, insert stopper), wipe outside, weigh → m₁

  3. 3

    Empty, dry the pycnometer completely.

  4. 4

    Fill with test liquid, insert stopper, wipe, weigh → m₂

  5. 5

    Note laboratory temperature.

  6. 6

    Calculate density using formula above.

Your Notebook Result
Weight of fluid = 945 grams → Density = 945 kg/m³
VIVA QUESTIONS & ANSWERS
    What is the difference between density and specific gravity? ▼
    Density = mass/volume (kg/m³ or g/cm³). Specific gravity (SG) = density of substance / density of water at 4°C. SG is dimensionless. For diesel: density ≈ 750 kg/m³, SG ≈ 0.75. For water: density = 1000 kg/m³, SG = 1.0.
    Why must the pycnometer be completely dry before filling with test liquid? ▼
    Any residual water would mix with the test liquid, diluting it and changing its actual density. This would give an incorrect mass reading and hence an incorrect density calculation.
    Why is temperature important in density measurement? ▼
    Density of most liquids changes with temperature (liquids expand when heated, reducing density). Standard density measurements are reported at 15°C or 20°C. Laboratory temperature must be recorded for accurate results.
EXP 09 Cloud Point & Pour Point Apparatus ▼
AIM & APPARATUS

Aim: To determine the cloud point and pour point of a given lubricant.


Apparatus: Cooling bath with stand, metal jar with bottom insulated disc, glass jar with rubber gasket and cork, thermometer, dry ice.

DEFINITIONS — MUST KNOW!
Cloud Point
The temperature at which a cloud or haze FIRST appears in the oil when it is cooled under prescribed conditions. Wax crystals begin to form.
Pour Point
The lowest temperature at which the oil is observed to flow when cooled and tilted. Pour Point = last temperature at which oil flows + 3°C correction. When the tilted surface shows no movement for 5 seconds → Pour Point.
🧠 Memory Trick

Cloud → Cloudy appearance (haze forms) — higher temp
Pour → Can you pour it? (flow stops) — lower temp
So: Cloud Point > Pour Point always.

STEP-BY-STEP PROCEDURE
  1. 1

    Keep the cooling bath on stand. Fill dry ice cubes around the bath up to neck level.

  2. 2

    Fill oil in glass tube up to lower marked line. Put rubber cork tightly. Place inside metal vessel.

  3. 3

    Fix thermometer in its position.

  4. 4

    Temperature will drop. Remove jar from jacket quickly and inspect oil for cloud (haze appearance). Record thermometer reading → this is Cloud Point.

  5. 5

    Close the jar and cool further. Inspect at every 2°C interval.

  6. 6

    Tilt the jar horizontally. If oil shows NO MOVEMENT for 5 seconds → record that temperature. Add 3°C → Pour Point.

  7. 7

    Repeat the test — results should not differ by more than 1°C.

RESULTS (From Notebook)
Cloud Point observation: 21°C + 10°C correction = 31°C
Pour Point observation: 18°C + 10°C correction = 28°C
VIVA QUESTIONS & ANSWERS
    What causes oil to have a cloud point? ▼
    Petroleum oils contain wax (paraffin compounds). As temperature drops, these wax compounds begin to crystallize and separate out of solution, making the oil appear cloudy or hazy. The Cloud Point marks the onset of this wax crystallization.
    What is the significance of pour point for lubricants? ▼
    Pour point determines the lowest ambient temperature at which the lubricant can be used. If the ambient temperature drops below the pour point, the oil will not flow — causing pump failure, inadequate lubrication at startup, and engine damage. Critical for cold-climate applications.
    Is pour point always lower than cloud point? ▼
    Yes, always. Cloud point is when wax STARTS forming (oil still flows). Pour point is when wax has formed enough to STOP flow. So: Cloud Point > Pour Point. Exception: waxy-free oils (naphthenic) may have very close or coincident values.
    How can the pour point of a lubricant be improved (lowered)? ▼
    By adding pour point depressants (PPD) — chemical additives that interfere with wax crystal growth, keeping the oil flowing at lower temperatures. Dewaxing (removing wax compounds) is another method used in refinery processes.
EXP 10 Rams Bottom Carbon Residue Apparatus ▼
AIM & APPARATUS

Aim: To determine the percentage of carbon residue of sample fuel oils.


Apparatus: Metal furnace with 5 wells, temperature control box, temperature sensor, cocking (coking) bulb, syringe for filling sample oil.

THEORY

Carbon Residue = the amount of solid carbonaceous matter (coke) left after evaporation and pyrolysis (thermal decomposition) of oil when heated under controlled conditions without air.


It is an important quality parameter — indicates the tendency of oil to form carbon deposits in engines, combustion chambers, and injectors. Lower carbon residue = better fuel quality.

  1. 1

    Take 2–4 grams of sample oil in a glass bulb (coking bulb) that has a capillary opening. Inject using syringe.

  2. 2

    Weigh the sample oil and also the coking bulb (before adding sample).

  3. 3

    Place the sample with bulb inside the round heating bath (furnace well).

  4. 4

    Set the metal furnace temperature between 300°C to 550°C as per test requirement using digital temperature controller.

  5. 5

    Keep bulb with metal tongs inside the bath for about 20 minutes until sample oil is completely burnt.

  6. 6

    After removal, cool the bulb and weigh to nearest 0.1 mg.

  7. 7

    Calculate carbon residue %.

VIVA QUESTIONS & ANSWERS
    What is carbon residue and why is it tested? ▼
    Carbon residue is the solid carbonaceous deposit remaining after evaporation and pyrolysis of a fuel or lubricant. It is tested to evaluate the tendency of oil to form coke/carbon deposits in engine parts like pistons, injectors, and combustion chambers. High carbon residue leads to fouling and reduced engine performance.
    What are the two standard methods to determine carbon residue? ▼
    (1) Conradson Carbon Residue (CCR) test — older method using a Conradson crucible. (2) Rams Bottom Carbon Residue (RBCR) test — modern, more precise method using a coking bulb in a controlled furnace. ASTM D524 standard for Rams Bottom method.
    What is pyrolysis? ▼
    Pyrolysis is the thermal decomposition of organic material at elevated temperatures (300–700°C) in the absence of oxygen. The material breaks down into gases, liquid (bio-oil), and solid (char/coke). In the carbon residue test, the oil undergoes pyrolysis, leaving behind solid carbon.
EXP 11 Pensky-Martens Flash Point Apparatus (Closed Cup) ▼
AIM & APPARATUS

Aim: To determine the flash point of the given oil using Pensky-Martens Closed Cup apparatus.


Apparatus: Pensky-Martens flash point apparatus, thermometer, test flame device, stirrer, sample oil.

KEY DEFINITIONS
Flash Point
The lowest temperature at which the vapours of a liquid form an ignitable mixture with air and produce a momentary flash on application of a small flame. It does NOT sustain combustion.
Fire Point (Different!)
The temperature at which the oil vapours SUSTAIN combustion for at least 5 seconds after ignition. Fire Point > Flash Point (typically 5–40°C higher).
🧠 Memory Trick

Flash = Momentary flash (blink!) — vapours ignite but don't sustain.
Fire = Sustained burning (stays lit!) — vapours keep burning.
Flash Point < Fire Point always.

STEP-BY-STEP PROCEDURE
  1. 1

    Fill the test cup with sample oil up to the marked line (not too full).

  2. 2

    Close the lid tightly (closed cup method). Insert thermometer.

  3. 3

    Heat the oil at a controlled rate (~5-6°C per minute) with continuous stirring.

  4. 4

    Apply the test flame at regular temperature intervals (every 1°C near expected flash point).

  5. 5

    The temperature at which a distinct flash (not sustained flame) first appears is the Flash Point.

  6. 6

    Record the thermometer reading immediately as flash point.

VIVA QUESTIONS & ANSWERS
    What is the significance of flash point? ▼
    Flash point indicates the volatility and fire hazard of a fuel or oil. Lower flash point = more volatile = higher fire risk. It is used for: (1) Safe storage and transportation regulations, (2) Classification of flammable liquids, (3) Selection of fuels and lubricants for specific applications.
    What is the difference between open cup and closed cup flash point tests? ▼
    Open Cup (Cleveland method): oil is heated in an open cup; vapours disperse freely, giving a higher flash point reading. Closed Cup (Pensky-Martens): oil is heated in a sealed cup; vapours concentrate, giving a LOWER flash point — more accurate for hazard assessment. Closed cup values are always lower than open cup values for the same oil.
    What are typical flash points for common fuels? ▼
    Petrol (Gasoline): −40°C (very dangerous!). Diesel: 52–80°C (your result: 54°C — matches!). Kerosene: 37–65°C. Lubricating oil: 200–300°C. The high flash point of diesel makes it safer to store than petrol.
    Why does diesel engine not need a spark plug? ▼
    Diesel fuel ignites by compression heat, not a spark. In a diesel engine, air is compressed to very high pressure (~16:1 to 22:1 CR), raising its temperature to 500–700°C. When diesel is injected, it spontaneously ignites due to this high temperature (auto-ignition). Diesel's auto-ignition temperature is ~250°C.
EXP 12 Bomb Calorimeter — Heat of Combustion ▼
AIM & APPARATUS

Aim: To understand the working of bomb calorimeter and measure the standard enthalpy of combustion of a sample. Use known heat of benzoic acid to determine heat capacity of calorimeter, then measure unknown sample.


Apparatus: Bomb calorimeter, oxygen cylinder, pellet press, fuse wire, thermometer, water bath, analytical balance.

THEORY

A bomb calorimeter measures heat released during combustion at constant volume. The temperature rise of the surrounding water is proportional to the heat of combustion of the sample.

Key Formula
Q = m_water × C_water × ΔT  (for calorimeter calibration)

CV (Calorific Value) = Q / mass of sample

Water Equivalent (WE) = Q / ΔT (accounts for heat absorbed by bomb itself)

CV = (WE × ΔT) / (mass of sample) kJ/kg
PROCEDURE
  1. 1

    Prepare sample as a pellet (using pellet press). Attach fuse wire through the pellet.

  2. 2

    Place pellet in the bomb crucible. Connect fuse wire to electrodes.

  3. 3

    Fill the bomb with oxygen at 20–30 atm pressure. Seal the bomb.

  4. 4

    Immerse bomb in water bath. Record initial temperature accurately every 30 seconds.

  5. 5

    Ignite sample by passing electric current through fuse wire.

  6. 6

    Record temperature rise until it stabilizes. Note maximum temperature.

  7. 7

    Calculate ΔT = T_max − T_initial

  8. 8

    Calculate heat released and calorific value.

VIVA QUESTIONS & ANSWERS
    Why is the bomb calorimeter called a "bomb"? ▼
    Because the steel vessel (bomb) is sealed and can withstand very high pressures generated during combustion (pressures up to 1000 atm). The word "bomb" refers to the explosive-proof nature of the vessel. Combustion occurs at constant VOLUME (unlike a boiler which is constant pressure).
    What is the difference between HCV and LCV (Gross and Net Calorific Value)? ▼
    HCV (Higher Calorific Value / Gross CV): heat released when products are cooled to standard temp, so water in products CONDENSES — includes latent heat. LCV (Lower CV / Net CV): water remains as vapour — latent heat not recovered. LCV = HCV − (latent heat × mass of water formed). In engines, LCV is used as fuel does not allow condensation.
    What is the purpose of using benzoic acid as the standard? ▼
    Benzoic acid has a precisely known, internationally certified calorific value (26,435 kJ/kg or 6318 cal/g). By burning it in the bomb, the actual heat capacity (water equivalent) of the calorimeter is determined. This calibration is then used to find the CV of the unknown sample.
    What is calorific value? Typical values for fuels? ▼
    Calorific value = heat released per unit mass of fuel on complete combustion (kJ/kg or Cal/g). Typical: Coal: 25,000–35,000 kJ/kg. Petrol: ~44,000 kJ/kg. Diesel: ~44,800 kJ/kg. LPG: ~46,000 kJ/kg. Biogas: ~20,000–25,000 kJ/m³. Natural gas: ~38,000 kJ/m³.
EXP 13 Torrefaction & Pyrolysis of Biomass ▼
INTRODUCTION

Torrefaction and Pyrolysis are thermochemical processes used to convert biomass into valuable energy products under controlled heating in the absence or limited presence of oxygen.

TORREFACTION vs PYROLYSIS — Side by Side
ParameterTorrefactionPyrolysis
Temperature200–300°C300–700°C

TORREFACTION — Key Details
  • Mild process: 200–300°C, O₂-free environment
  • Removes moisture and light volatile compounds
  • Product: dry, brittle, energy-dense solid called "torrefied biomass" or "bio-coal"
  • Enhances: calorific value, grindability, hydrophobicity (water resistance)
  • Makes biomass suitable for storage, transport, and co-firing with coal in power plants
PYROLYSIS — Key Details
  • More extensive: 300–700°C, no oxygen
  • Unlike torrefaction, pyrolysis BREAKS DOWN biomass into THREE products
  • Three products: (1) Solid = Biochar, (2) Liquid = Bio-oil, (3) Gas = Syngas/Syngas
  • Slow pyrolysis: maximizes biochar production
  • Fast pyrolysis: maximizes bio-oil yield (up to 75%)
  • Distribution of products depends on temperature, heating rate, and residence time
💡 Summary for Both Processes

Both play a significant role in sustainable energy — enabling efficient utilization of biomass and reducing dependence on fossil fuels.

VIVA QUESTIONS & ANSWERS
    What is the difference between torrefaction and pyrolysis? ▼
    Temperature: Torrefaction = 200–300°C (mild). Pyrolysis = 300–700°C (intensive). Products: Torrefaction gives ONE solid product (bio-coal). Pyrolysis gives THREE products: biochar (solid), bio-oil (liquid), syngas (gas). Both occur in absence of oxygen but pyrolysis decomposes biomass more completely.
    What is biochar and what are its uses? ▼
    Biochar is the solid carbon-rich product of pyrolysis. Uses: (1) Soil amendment — improves water retention and nutrient availability, (2) Carbon sequestration — locks carbon in soil for long periods, (3) Energy source — has moderate calorific value, (4) Pollutant removal — activated biochar can adsorb heavy metals.
    What is the difference between slow and fast pyrolysis? ▼
    Slow pyrolysis: low heating rate (1–10°C/min), long residence time → maximizes BIOCHAR yield (~35%). Fast pyrolysis: very high heating rate (1000°C/s), short residence time → maximizes BIO-OIL yield (~75%). Flash pyrolysis is even faster and gives higher bio-oil yield.
    Why is torrefied biomass better than raw biomass for co-firing? ▼
    Torrefied biomass has: (1) Higher energy density (similar to lignite), (2) Lower moisture content — less energy wasted evaporating water, (3) Better grindability — can be pulverized like coal, (4) Hydrophobic — easier to store outdoors, (5) Higher calorific value per kg. All these properties make it compatible with existing coal handling infrastructure.
EXP 14 Gasification ▼
INTRODUCTION

Gasification is a thermochemical process that converts carbon-based materials (biomass, coal, waste) into a combustible gas mixture called Syngas (synthetic gas) by reacting at high temperatures (700–1200°C) with a controlled amount of oxygen, air, or steam.

THE FOUR STAGES OF GASIFICATION
StageProcessWhat happens
1. Drying100–200°CMoisture removed from biomass
2. Pyrolysis200–500°CBiomass decomposes into char, tar, and gases
3. Oxidation>700°CPartial combustion provides heat for process: C + O₂ → CO₂, 2C + O₂ → 2CO
4. Reduction700–1000°CCO₂ + C → 2CO; H₂O + C → CO + H₂ (Syngas produced here)
🧠 Remember the 4 Stages

"Dry Pizza Ooooh Reduce!" → D-P-O-R → Drying, Pyrolysis, Oxidation, Reduction

KEY ADVANTAGES OF GASIFICATION
  • Produces clean, combustible gas (Syngas: CO + H₂) for power generation and heating
  • Higher efficiency compared to direct combustion
  • Lower emissions and better environmental performance
  • Can use various feedstocks: biomass, municipal waste, coal
  • Reduces landfill waste — supports waste-to-energy systems
  • Important for sustainable and renewable energy applications
VIVA QUESTIONS & ANSWERS
    What is gasification? How is it different from combustion? ▼
    Gasification: Partial oxidation of biomass at high temperature to produce syngas (CO + H₂). Controlled oxygen supply (less than stoichiometric). Product is a combustible gas, not heat directly. Combustion: Complete oxidation, excess oxygen, product is CO₂ + H₂O + heat directly. Gasification produces a versatile fuel gas that can be used in engines, turbines, or further processed.
    What is syngas? What is it composed of? ▼
    Syngas (synthesis gas or producer gas) mainly consists of: Carbon monoxide (CO) ~20–25%, Hydrogen (H₂) ~15–20%, Methane (CH₄) ~2–4%, CO₂ ~5–10%, N₂ (balance if air is used). Calorific value: ~4–6 MJ/m³ (much less than natural gas at 38 MJ/m³). Can be used in IC engines, gas turbines, or for chemical synthesis.
    What is the reduction zone and why is it important? ▼
    The reduction zone (700–1000°C) is the most important stage where the actual syngas is produced. CO₂ and H₂O react with hot char: C + CO₂ → 2CO (Boudouard reaction) and C + H₂O → CO + H₂ (water-gas reaction). These endothermic reactions consume heat from the oxidation zone above.
EXP 15 Biogas Plant ▼
INTRODUCTION

A biogas plant produces biogas through anaerobic digestion — a biological process carried out by microorganisms in the absence of oxygen. Feedstocks: animal dung, agricultural residues, kitchen waste.


It is an ecofriendly, renewable method of energy generation, widely used in rural and semi-urban areas.

  • CO₂: 30–45%
  • Small amounts of H₂S and other gases
  • Methane is responsible for energy generation
APPLICATIONS & ADVANTAGES
  • Cooking, lighting, electricity generation, heating

  • Reduces greenhouse gas emissions

  • Cost-effective, easy to operate, suitable for rural households and farms
VIVA QUESTIONS & ANSWERS
    What is anaerobic digestion? Why must it be in absence of oxygen? ▼
    Anaerobic digestion is a biological process where microorganisms break down organic matter in the absence of oxygen, producing biogas (mainly CH₄). Oxygen is excluded because: (1) Methane-producing bacteria (methanogens) are strict anaerobes — oxygen kills them, (2) With oxygen, aerobic bacteria would dominate and produce CO₂ + water instead of methane.
    What are the differences between biogas, syngas, and natural gas? ▼
    Biogas: ~60% CH₄ + 40% CO₂, produced by anaerobic digestion of organic waste, low pressure, CV ≈ 20–25 MJ/m³. Syngas: CO + H₂ mixture, produced by gasification, CV ≈ 4–6 MJ/m³. Natural gas: 90%+ CH₄, fossil fuel, extracted from underground, CV ≈ 38 MJ/m³. Biogas is renewable; natural gas is not.
    What factors affect biogas production? ▼
    (1) Temperature: optimal 35–37°C (mesophilic) or 55°C (thermophilic). (2) pH: optimal 6.8–7.2. (3) C:N ratio: optimal 20–30:1. (4) Retention time: 20–30 days typically. (5) Mixing: regular stirring improves contact. (6) Absence of inhibitors: heavy metals, antibiotics, disinfectants can kill bacteria.
    What is the difference between a floating drum and fixed dome biogas plant? ▼
    Floating drum (KVIC design): metal drum floats on slurry, moves up-down as gas accumulates, constant pressure, visible gas storage. Fixed dome (Deenbandhu design): fixed underground dome, variable pressure, no moving parts, more durable, cheaper, but pressure varies. Fixed dome is more common in India due to lower cost and maintenance.
